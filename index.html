Research Experience

Project Details

The focus of my research falls under the category of mesh networks, network security, and layer 3 attacks. Specifically, I am investigating Blackhole attacks, which is a form of layer 3 DoS attacks which can disrupt network reliability.

What Is A MANET?

Mobile Adhoc Networks (MANETs) is an under researched approach to wireless network architectures. There are a few key differences that separates MANETs from open wireless networks. The first being, that nodes have the ability to join the network and use network resources without approval from a central authority. Additionally, nodes can leave the network without much impact to efficiency and reliability. Another important feature of a MANET is that nodes act as routers. In a traditional wireless network, devices act as a client share the network resources. In MANETs, nodes play a role in routing information through the network. Lastly, the network is self-sustaining as nodes in this network are meant to be mobile, the network architecture is constantly adapting to changes within the network.

What Is A Blackhole Attack?

For an efficient operation of a MANET (Mobile Ad Hoc Network), establishing a baseline level of trust among its nodes is essential. However, this inherent trust can be vulnerable to exploitation by malicious nodes. MANETs, like traditional networks, are susceptible to layer 3 attacks. The unique challenge in MANETs arises from the active involvement of nodes in routing information, allowing a malicious node the capability to intentionally drop all packets directed towards it. This form of attack is known as a Blackhole attack, posing a significant threat to the network's reliability and integrity.


Mitigation Strategies For Blackhole Attacks

Various methods have been proposed for detecting and mitigating Blackhole attacks. One brute-force strategy involves forwarding traffic to a neighbor and switching to another if no response is received. Another approach entails incorporating additional ACK messages, with nodes along the path sending ACK messages to the origin node, thereby informing it of undropped packets. However, the most efficient method appears to be maintaining a memory-based table that monitors the trust level between known neighboring nodes. In this approach, successful packet transmissions between two nodes result in an increment of trust value between them.
